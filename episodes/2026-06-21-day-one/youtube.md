# Episode 1: Day Zero — I'm Building Something. I Don't Know What Yet.
Date: 2026-06-13
Format: YouTube (~10–12 min)

---

## Script

> **Production note:** Mix of talking head + text overlays + screen recording. Pace: natural, direct — like talking to a friend, not presenting. Pause after punchy lines. No music under talking head sections.

---

**[HOOK — direct to camera, no smile, just presence]**

Okay, so I'm going to tell you something that sounds a little backwards.

I decided to build a micro-SaaS. I've already started documenting it. And I still don't have the idea.

I know. I know how that sounds.

But here's the thing — most developers wait, right? They want the idea first, then the validation, then some big plan, then finally they build. Documentation — if it even happens — comes at the very end, after everything worked out.

I'm doing this differently. I'm starting before I've figured anything out. Right now. On camera.

**[TEXT OVERLAY — slams in fast, holds 2s]**
"No idea. No roadmap. No excuse."

This is day zero.

---

**[WHO THIS IS FOR]**

If you've ever thought about building something — a SaaS product, a developer tool, some side project that could actually make money — but you kept waiting. Kept telling yourself "not yet, not ready, not sure enough." This one's for you.

I'm not here to pretend I have it figured out. I genuinely don't. And I think watching someone navigate the messy beginning — honestly, on camera — is way more useful than watching someone show you a finished thing and then reverse-engineer the story.

---

**[SECTION 1: Why I Made This Decision]**

I've been a developer for years. I've had ideas. Loads of them. I've also never shipped a product that made money. Not a single one.

And it's not like I didn't try. I've started things. Built prototypes. I have notes files — long ones — just full of "this could be something."

But the pattern is always the same, right? Idea comes, I get excited, I think about it for two weeks, and then I slowly convince myself — it's not different enough, market's too competitive, I don't have time to do this properly. And I drop it.

The problem isn't the ideas. The problem is that gap — between getting an idea and actually committing to it.

So this time I'm doing something different. I'm committing to the process before I commit to the idea.

What does that look like practically?

It means building in public. Every decision I make, every direction I explore, every dead end I hit, every moment where I have no clue what I'm doing — all of it goes on camera.

The goal isn't a perfect story at the end. The goal is to capture the real story while it's actually happening. Including the parts that don't go well.

**[TEXT OVERLAY — centered, bold, holds 2s]**
"Commit to the process. Not the idea."

---

**[SECTION 2: Why Micro-SaaS]**

Let me explain what I mean by micro-SaaS, because it's a specific thing — it's not just any software startup.

A micro-SaaS is a software product — usually subscription-based — built and run by one person. No team. No investors. No product manager standing over your shoulder. One developer, one problem, one type of customer.

And the products that actually work in this space? They're narrow. They solve one specific problem that one specific kind of person keeps having. Not a platform. Not an ecosystem. Just one thing, done really well, that someone will pay a small monthly fee to use.

I've seen this work. Developers making $500 a month, $2,000 a month, sometimes more — from a single tool. Running mostly on autopilot. Funded entirely by subscriptions. One person. No boss. No VC calls.

That's what I'm aiming for. Not a startup. Not a venture-backed thing. Something real, something I built, something that solves an actual problem.

The reason I'm going micro-SaaS specifically — not mobile apps, not a marketplace, not content — is that it's the model that fits what I can actually do alone. I'm a developer. I can build software. I don't want to manage a team. I want to build something valuable, own it completely, and make it sustainable.

**[TEXT OVERLAY]**
"One developer. One problem. One customer type."

---

**[SECTION 3: Using AI to Find the Idea]**

**[SCREEN RECORDING — Claude conversation open, scrolling slowly through idea exploration, 6–8s]**

Okay, so this is the part where it gets a bit different.

I'm using AI to help me figure out what to build.

Not to generate some list of business ideas — I've read those lists. "Build a project management tool." "Create an invoicing app." Completely useless. The idea isn't the hard part. Knowing which idea is actually worth your time — that's the hard part.

I'm using AI differently. As a thinking partner.

I opened Claude — that's Anthropic's AI — and I just started talking through what I actually know. What problems I've run into in my own work. What things I still do manually that probably shouldn't be manual. What tools I've wished existed. What workarounds I've built for myself that other people probably need too.

And then I asked it to push back on me. What's the real problem here? Who else has this? Is it painful enough that someone would actually pay? Has someone already built this? What makes it hard to build? What makes it hard to sell?

**[TEXT OVERLAY over screen recording]**
"Using AI to pressure-test the idea. Not generate it."

That whole conversation — I've linked it in the description. Real one, unedited. You'll see exactly where I was thinking clearly and where I was hand-waving and hoping Claude wouldn't notice.

This is what using AI to think actually looks like before you have a product. It's not magic. It's just a structured conversation. But it moves faster than thinking alone, and it asks questions I'd conveniently skip if I was just journaling by myself.

---

**[SECTION 4: Where I Am Right Now — The Honest Version]**

Let me be straight about what day zero actually looks like.

I don't have a validated idea. I have directions.

A few problem spaces that feel worth exploring. Things I've noticed in my own work that might be worth building for. Questions I need to answer before I can commit to any of them. No paying customers. No prototype. No launch date.

What I do have is a process for getting there.

Over the next few episodes, I'm going to work through all of that on camera. What makes a micro-SaaS idea worth pursuing? What makes it a waste of time? How do you test demand before writing a single line of code? How do you find potential customers and actually talk to them?

All of that comes before I build anything. And all of it goes on camera.

---

**[SECTION 5: Why Document From Day Zero — Not Day One]**

One more thing — because I've thought about this a lot.

I could've waited. Found the idea first, validated it, built something, then started filming. A lot of creators do exactly that — they have the full story ready before they start telling it.

But I think that misses the most valuable part.

The part nobody documents is the beginning. The messy, uncomfortable part. That "I think this might be something but I'm not sure" phase. When you're weighing three different directions and genuinely don't know which one is right. When you almost talk yourself out of it completely.

That's the part that's hardest to navigate. And watching someone go through it in real time — not polished in retrospect, not cleaned up after the fact — that's more useful than any highlight reel.

Most content about building products just skips straight to the insight. "Here's what I learned about finding product-market fit." But it cuts out the weeks of confusion that came before any clarity showed up.

I want to capture the confusion. That's what starting something actually looks like.

**[TEXT OVERLAY — holds 2.5s]**
"The confusion is the content."

---

**[LESSON — lean slightly forward]**

Here's what I already know after day zero.

Starting before you're ready isn't the mistake. Waiting until you're ready — that's the mistake.

Every developer I know who actually shipped something that earns money — they didn't wait until it was perfectly figured out. They started with a direction, built the smallest thing that could answer a real question, and kept adjusting from there.

If I document all of this and the product fails, the documentation is still worth something. If I wait until I have it figured out — I might wait forever.

I'd rather have a documented failure than a perfect idea I never started.
