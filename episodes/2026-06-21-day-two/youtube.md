# Episode 1: Day One — I Am Building Something. I Don't Know What Yet.
Date: 2026-06-21
Format: YouTube (~10–12 min)

---

## Script

> **Production note:** Talking head with text overlays and brief screen recording. Speak at a calm, natural pace. Pause after key lines. No background music under talking head sections.

---

**[HOOK — direct to camera, calm and decided]**

I am a software developer.

I have been writing code for years.

And I have never built anything that made me money outside my job.

Not one thing.

Today, that changes.

And I am filming it.

**[TEXT OVERLAY — holds 2s]**
"No idea. No plan. Just the decision."

---

**[PERSONAL STORY]**

**[CAMERA — talking head, relaxed]**

In 2021, I moved from India to the United Kingdom.

I came here to build a better life. For my wife. For my daughter.

I found a good job as a full-stack developer. Things are going well.

But I always had this feeling.

I want to build something of my own.

Not just for the money. But to prove to myself that I can do it.

I have tried before.

I get an idea. I get excited. I think about it for two or three weeks.

And then I start finding reasons to stop.

"The market is too competitive." "I don't have enough time." "Someone already built this."

And then I go back to my job. And I forget about it.

This has happened many times.

I have notes files with ideas going back years. Long ones. Nothing ever shipped.

So this time, I am trying something different.

I am not waiting until I have the idea figured out.

I am starting now. And I am doing it on camera.

---

**[SECTION 1: What I Am Doing Differently]**

**[CAMERA — talking head]**

Most people build first. Then they document.

They wait until they have something to show. Then they start talking about it.

By then, the story is already cleaned up. The messy parts are gone.

I want to go the other way.

I am starting the camera before I have the idea.

Because when I watch other people's content, the part I find most useful is the beginning.

When they are confused. When they don't know what to do next. When they are figuring it out in real time.

That is the part that helps me.

And that is the part nobody films.

So I decided — I am going to film it.

All of it.

The confusion. The wrong turns. The moments where I have no idea what I am doing.

**[TEXT OVERLAY — holds 2s]**
"Commit to the process. Not the idea."

I don't have a validated idea yet.

I have some directions. Some ideas I want to explore.

But I am starting now. Before I have everything figured out.

---

**[SECTION 2: What Is Micro-SaaS]**

**[CAMERA — talking head]**

Let me explain what micro-SaaS means. Because it is a specific thing.

A micro-SaaS is a small software product.

People usually pay for it every month.

Built and run by one person.

No team. No investors. No office.

One developer, one problem, one type of customer.

And these products work.

I have seen developers making £500 a month, £2,000 a month, sometimes more. From a single tool. Running mostly by itself. One person.

That is what I want to build.

Not a big startup. Not something with investors.

Just something real. Something I built. Something that solves one specific problem for one specific person.

And earns a small amount every month.

**[TEXT OVERLAY]**
"One developer. One problem. One customer."

I am a developer. I know how to build software.

I don't want to manage a team. I don't want investors telling me what to build.

I want to own something I created.

That is why I chose this path.

---

**[SECTION 3: How I Am Using AI to Find the Idea]**

**[SCREEN RECORDING — Claude conversation open, scrolling slowly, 6–8s]**

Here is where it gets interesting.

I am using AI to help me think.

Not to give me a list of ideas.

I have read those lists. "Build a project management tool." "Make an invoicing app."

They don't help.

Anyone can think of an idea. That is not the hard part.

The hard part is knowing which idea is actually worth your time.

So I opened Claude — that is Anthropic's AI — and I just started talking.

I told it about problems I have seen in my own work.

Things I still do by hand that probably should not be manual.

Tools I wished existed.

And then I asked it to push back on me.

Is this a real problem? Is it painful enough that someone would pay for it? Has someone already built this? What makes it hard to sell?

**[TEXT OVERLAY over screen recording]**
"Using AI to test the idea. Not to invent it."

That whole conversation is in the description. The real one. Unedited.

You will see exactly where I was thinking clearly.

And where I was just hoping Claude wouldn't ask the next question.

This is what using AI to think actually looks like.

It is not magic. It is just a structured conversation.

But it moves faster than thinking alone.

And it asks questions I would normally skip.

---

**[SECTION 4: Where I Am Right Now — The Honest Version]**

**[CAMERA — direct, honest]**

Let me be straight about where I am today.

I do not have an idea I'm sure about yet.

I have some directions. A few ideas that feel worth exploring.

I have questions I need to answer before I can commit to any of them.

No customers. No prototype. No launch date.

What I do have is a process. And a camera.

Over the next few episodes, I am going to work through all of this here.

What makes a micro-SaaS idea worth building?

What makes it a waste of time?

How do you test if people want it — before you write a single line of code?

How do you find real customers and actually talk to them?

All of that comes before I build anything.

And all of it goes on camera.

---

**[SECTION 5: Why Start From Day One — Not After]**

**[CAMERA — leaning slightly forward]**

I want to say one more thing. Because I thought about this a lot.

I could have waited.

Found the idea first. Validated it. Built something. Then started filming.

A lot of people do exactly that.

They have the full story ready before they start telling it.

But I think that misses the most valuable part.

The part nobody documents is the beginning.

The part where you don't know what you are building or who it is for.

When you are weighing three different directions and you don't know which one is right.

When you almost talk yourself out of it completely.

That is the part that is hardest to navigate.

And watching someone go through it in real time — honestly, not cleaned up afterwards — that is more useful than any highlight video.

Most content about building products jumps straight to the lesson.

"Here is what I learned about finding customers."

But it skips the weeks of confusion that came before.

I want to capture the confusion.

That is what starting something actually looks like.

**[TEXT OVERLAY — holds 2.5s]**
"The confusion is the content."

---

**[LESSON — calm, direct]**

Here is what I have already learned. Just from making this decision.

Waiting until I am ready was never going to work.

Every developer I know who actually shipped something that earns money — they didn't wait for the perfect moment.

They started with a direction.

They built the smallest thing they could to answer a real question.

And they kept adjusting.

If I document all of this and the product fails — the documentation is still worth something.

If I keep waiting until I have it all figured out — I might wait forever.

I would rather have a documented failure than a perfect idea I never started.

---

**[SIMPLE ENDING — direct to camera]**

If you want to watch this happen in real time — the whole journey, from idea to build to launch — subscribe.

Next episode: I am evaluating the idea.

I will show you the exact questions I am asking, the framework I am using, and the direction I am going to commit to.

See you there.
