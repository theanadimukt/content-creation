# Episode 1: Day Zero — I'm Building Something. I Don't Know What Yet.
Date: 2026-06-13
Format: YouTube (~10–12 min)

---

## Script

> **Production note:** Mix of talking head + text overlays + screen recording. Pace: deliberate, confident — no filler. Pause after short punchy lines. No background music needed for talking head sections.

**[HOOK — direct to camera, no smile, just presence]**

I'm going to tell you something that sounds backwards.

I decided to build a micro-SaaS. I've already started documenting it. And I still don't have the idea.

Most developers wait. They want the idea first, then the validation, then the plan, then the build. The documentation — if it happens at all — comes at the end, after everything worked out.

I'm doing this differently. I'm starting before I have anything figured out. Right now. On camera.

**[TEXT OVERLAY — slams in fast, holds 2s]**
"No idea. No roadmap. No excuse."

This is day zero.

---

**[WHO THIS IS FOR]**

If you've ever thought about building something — a SaaS product, a developer tool, a side project that could make real money — but you kept waiting until you had it figured out, this is for you.

I'm not here to pretend I have it figured out. I don't. And I think watching someone navigate the beginning honestly is more useful than watching someone present a finished thing and reverse-engineer the story.

---

**[SECTION 1: Why I Made This Decision]**

I've been a developer for years. I've had ideas. Lots of them. I've also never shipped a product that made money.

Not for lack of trying. I've started things. Built prototypes. Had long notes files full of "this could be something."

But the pattern is always the same. I get an idea, I get excited, I think about it for two weeks, I convince myself it's not differentiated enough, or the market is too competitive, or I don't have time to do it right. And I drop it.

The problem isn't the ideas. The problem is the gap between getting an idea and committing to one.

So this time I'm trying something different. I'm going to commit to the process before I commit to the idea.

What does that mean in practice?

It means I'm building in public. Every decision I make, every direction I explore, every dead end I hit, every moment where I'm not sure what I'm doing — all of it goes on camera.

The goal isn't to have a perfect story to tell you when I'm done. The goal is to capture the real story while it's happening. Including the parts that don't go well.

**[TEXT OVERLAY — centered, bold, holds 2s]**
"Commit to the process. Not the idea."

---

**[SECTION 2: Why Micro-SaaS]**

I should explain what I mean by micro-SaaS, because it's a specific thing.

A micro-SaaS is a software product — usually subscription-based — built and maintained by one person. No team. No investors. No product manager. Just one developer, one problem, one type of customer.

The products that work in this space are narrow. They solve a specific problem that a specific kind of person has repeatedly. Not a platform. Not an ecosystem. One thing, done well, that someone will pay a small monthly fee to access.

I've seen this work. Developers building tools that generate $500 a month, $2,000 a month, sometimes much more. Running largely on autopilot. Funded entirely by subscriptions. One person, no dependency on an employer, no raising capital.

That's the shape of thing I'm aiming at. Not a startup. Not a venture-scale business. Something real, something I built, something that solves a problem someone will pay to have solved.

The reason I'm targeting micro-SaaS specifically — and not a mobile app, not a marketplace, not a content business — is that it's the model that best fits what I can do alone. I'm a developer. I can build software. I don't want to manage a team. I want to build something valuable and sustainable that I own outright.

**[TEXT OVERLAY]**
"One developer. One problem. One customer type."

---

**[SECTION 3: Using AI to Find the Idea]**

**[SCREEN RECORDING — Claude conversation open, idea exploration visible, scrolling slowly, 6–8s]**

Here's the part that makes this a bit different.

I'm using AI to help me figure out what to build.

Not to generate a list of business ideas. I've read those lists. "Build a project management tool." "Create an invoicing app." They're not useful. The idea isn't the hard part — knowing which idea is worth your time is the hard part.

I'm using AI differently. I'm using it as a thinking partner.

I opened a conversation with Claude — Anthropic's AI — and started talking through what I actually know. What problems I've encountered in my own work. What things I do manually that feel like they should be automated. What tools I've wished existed. What workarounds I've built for myself that other people probably also need.

And then I asked it to push back. What's the actual problem here? Who else has this? Is it painful enough that someone would pay for a solution? Have other people already built this? What would make it hard to build? What would make it hard to sell?

**[TEXT OVERLAY over screen recording]**
"Using AI to pressure-test the idea. Not generate it."

That conversation is linked in the description — the real one, unedited. You'll see where I was thinking clearly and where I was hand-waving.

This is what using AI to think looks like before you have a product. It's not magic. It's a structured conversation. But it moves faster than thinking alone, and it asks questions I'd skip if I were just journaling.

---

**[SECTION 4: Where I Am Right Now — The Honest Version]**

Let me be straight about what day zero actually looks like.

I don't have a validated idea. I have directions.

I have a few problem spaces I think are worth exploring. Things I've noticed in my work that might be worth building for. I have questions I need to answer before I commit to any of them. I have no paying customers, no prototype, no launch date.

What I do have is a process for getting there.

Over the next few episodes, I'm going to work through the evaluation on camera. What makes a micro-SaaS idea worth pursuing? What makes it a waste of time? How do I test demand before writing code? How do I find potential customers and actually talk to them?

All of that happens before I build anything. And all of it goes on camera.

---

**[SECTION 5: Why Document From Day Zero — Not Day One]**

One more thing I want to address, because I've thought about this.

I could have waited. I could have found the idea first, validated it, built something real, and then started filming. A lot of content creators do exactly that — they have the story before they start telling it.

But I think that misses the most valuable part.

The part nobody documents is the beginning. The messy part. The "I think this might be something but I'm not sure" phase. The part where you're evaluating three different directions and you don't know which one is right. The part where you almost talk yourself out of it.

That's the part that's hardest to navigate. And watching someone navigate it in real time — not in retrospect, not cleaned up after the fact — is more useful than watching a highlight reel.

Most content about building products skips straight to the insight. "Here's what I learned about finding product-market fit." But it leaves out the weeks of confusion that came before the clarity.

I want to capture the confusion. That's what starting something actually looks like.

**[TEXT OVERLAY — holds 2.5s]**
"The confusion is the content."

---

**[LESSON — lean slightly forward]**

Here's what I already know after day zero.

Starting before you're ready isn't a mistake. Waiting until you're ready is.

Every developer I know who shipped something that earns real money didn't wait until they had it perfectly figured out. They started with a direction, built the minimum thing that could answer a real question, and kept adjusting.

If I document this whole thing and the product fails, the documentation is still valuable. If I wait until I have it figured out, I might wait forever.

I'd rather have a documented failure than a perfect idea I never started.
